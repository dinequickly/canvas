# Assessment Patterns for Calculus

Use these patterns when building exercises, quizzes, or tests:

1. Concept check
- Ask for interpretation of a graph, derivative sign, or limit behavior in plain language.
- Good for warm-ups and early-page checkpoints.

2. Worked procedure
- Have the learner compute a derivative, evaluate a limit, or analyze a critical point with shown steps.
- Good when fluency matters.

3. Error analysis
- Present a short incorrect solution and ask the learner to identify the mistake.
- Useful for quotient rule, chain rule, implicit differentiation, and optimization setup.

4. Modeling prompt
- Translate a verbal situation into a function, rate, or optimization expression before solving.
- Good for applications and synthesis.

5. Visual reasoning
- Pair a graph or Desmos embed with questions about slope, concavity, accumulation, or end behavior.
- Keep prompts focused on observation first, then computation.

Recommended page rhythm:
- 2 to 3 warm-up or retrieval items
- 2 worked examples
- 3 to 6 practice questions with increasing difficulty
- 1 reflection, summary, or exit ticket

Answer key guidance:
- For low-stakes practice, include full or partial solution steps.
- For quizzes, concise final answers plus one sentence of justification is often enough.
